What is our blog about?
Our blog “Ha-tom?” is all about exploring the fascinating world of atoms, subatomic particles, and matter. We created it to share curiosity and guide readers through the science behind everything around us — from the everyday objects we see to the invisible particles that hold the universe together.


What is the content of our blog?

• Matter: We begin with the basics, explaining what matter is and why it’s the foundation of everything.

• States of Matter: We show how matter exists as solids, liquids, gases, and plasma, and how these states change.

• Atom and Subatomic Particles: We introduce the atom and its parts — protons, neutrons, and electrons — and explain their roles.

• Atomic Models: We walk through the history of atomic models, from Dalton’s early ideas to the quantum model, showing how science evolved.

• What Holds Atoms Together?: We explain the forces inside atoms, like the strong nuclear force and electromagnetic force, that keep them stable.

• Importance of Subatomic Particles: We highlight why these particles matter — not just in science, but in life and technology.

• Applications in Real Life: We connect atomic science to everyday uses like electricity, medical imaging, nuclear energy, and even space exploration.

General Overview
Subatomic particles are the tiniest parts that make up everything around us. They are smaller than atoms and are the reason matter exists and behaves the way it does. These particles carry properties like mass and charge, which affect how they interact with one another. Scientists study them to understand why objects are solid, why electricity flows, and even how stars shine. In simple terms, subatomic particles are the invisible “building blocks” that explain the structure of matter and the forces that shape our universe.

MATTER

Matter is anything that takes up space and has mass. This means that matter occupies room in the universe and contains material that can be measured. Everything around us, whether we can see it or not, is made of matter. For example, a rock is matter because it feels heavy and takes up space, while water is matter because it can be weighed and fills a container. Even air, though invisible, is matter because it fills balloons and rooms and has measurable mass.



Matter has 4 existing states: solid, liquid, gas, and plasma. Solid has a defined shape and volume. Particles within a solid are packed very close together fixed in an ordered arrangement like a table, chair and rock. Liquid state is defined to have volume but lacks a definite shape. Particles within a liquid are not packed as close together as in a solid, allowing them to slide against each other. Examples of liquids include water, oil, and alcohol.A gas lacks either a defined shape or volume. Gas particles are widely separated. Examples of gases include air and the helium in a balloon. Like a gas, a plasma lacks a defined shape or volume. However, the particles of a plasma are electrically charged and are separated by vast differences. Examples of plasma include lightning and the aurora.



These states of matter, when zoomed in, are made up of smaller components. In the figure above, matter is shown to be composed of molecules. A molecule is the smallest unit of a substance, formed when two or more atoms bond together, and it retains the chemical properties of that substance. Water, rocks, air, and even neon lights are all made up of molecules. These molecules, in turn, are made up of smaller particles called atoms. Atoms are the fundamental building blocks of all matter, and they are further composed of subatomic particles such as protons, neutrons, and electrons.



ATOM AND ITS SUBATOMIC PARTICLES


An atom is the smallest unit of matter that retains all of the chemical properties of an element. This is a microscopic structure characterized by a dense, positively charged central core surrounded by a vast cloud of rapidly moving, negatively charged particles. While an atom is the smallest unit that retains an element's properties, it is not indivisible. It is composed of three primary subatomic particles, known as the proton, neutron, and electron.



The figure above shows how the subatomic particles are arranged in an atom. At its center lies the nucleus, which contains nearly all of the atom's mass concentrated into an incredibly tiny volume. Surrounding this nucleus is the electron cloud, an area of mostly empty space where extremely low-mass particles move dynamically in distinct energy levels. The overall structure is naturally balanced in a neutral state, where the positive charge of the core perfectly offsets the negative charge of the surrounding cloud.



STRUCTURE OF SUBATOMIC PARTICLE
Proton

The proton is a stable subatomic particle located within the atomic nucleus. It carries a positive elementary charge (+1e). The number of protons in a nucleus is the fundamental characteristic that defines an element's identity (its atomic number). Its mass is 1 atomic mass unit (amu).


Neutron

The neutron is a neutral subatomic particle that resides in the nucleus alongside the proton. It is denoted by n or n0. The neutron mass is slightly higher than the proton mass, which is 1 atomic mass unit. The charge of the neutron is null, which implies that the neutron is electrically neutral. Bound by the strong nuclear force, neutrons provide the necessary stability within the nucleus, acting as a buffer to counteract the electrostatic repulsion between positively charged protons.


Electron

The electron is a fundamental subatomic particle that exists outside the nucleus, occupying the vast space known as the electron cloud. It carries a negative elementary charge (-1e), perfectly balancing the charge of a proton. It has a relative mass of 0.00055 or 5.45 × 10-4 amu. Because they reside on the outermost boundaries of the atom, electrons are the particles responsible for chemical bonding and electricity.

Key Points:
Atom - is the smallest unit of matter, and is made up of three subatomic particles known as the proton, neutron, and electron.
Subatomic Particles Symbol Mass (amu) Charge Location
Proton p+ 1 +1 Inside the nucleus
Neutron e- 1 0 Inside the nucleus
Electron n0 5.45 x 10-4 -1 Outside the nucleus

ATOMIC MODELS


An atomic model is a scientific representation used to explain the structure of an atom. Since atoms are too small to be seen directly, scientists developed models based on observations, experiments, and evidence. The atomic model changed over time as new discoveries were made, from the early idea of atoms as solid particles to the modern quantum model that describes electrons in probability regions.


Dalton’s Solid Sphere Model

The first major scientific atomic model was developed by John Dalton in the early 1800s. Dalton transformed the ancient idea of atoms into a scientific theory supported by observations in chemistry. He proposed that all matter is made of extremely small particles called atoms. In his model, atoms were imagined as solid, indivisible spheres.



Dalton’s model became important because it helped explain how elements combine to form compounds. He suggested that atoms of the same element have the same mass and properties, while atoms of different elements have different masses and properties. He also explained that atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios during chemical reactions. This helped support ideas such as the law of conservation of mass, the law of definite proportions, and the law of multiple proportions.

In simple terms, Dalton pictured the atom as a tiny solid ball. This model was useful during his time because it gave scientists a clearer way to explain chemical reactions. However, it had one major limitation: it did not explain that atoms are made of smaller particles. Later discoveries showed that atoms are not indivisible after all.

Key Points of Dalton's model:
• Matter is made of tiny particles called atoms.
• Atoms are solid and indivisible.
• Atoms of the same element are alike.
• Atoms of different elements are different.
• Atoms combine in fixed ratios to form compounds.



Thomson’s Plum Pudding Model

Dalton’s model was later challenged when scientists discovered that atoms contain charged particles. In 1897, J.J. Thomson discovered the electron, a negatively charged particle. This discovery proved that atoms were not simple solid spheres. Instead, atoms had smaller parts inside them.

To explain the presence of electrons, Thomson supported the Plum Pudding Model. In this model, the atom was imagined as a positively charged sphere with negatively charged electrons embedded throughout it. The model is often compared to a pudding with plums or raisins scattered inside. The positive part represented the main body of the atom, while the electrons represented the negative particles placed within it.



This model was a major improvement because it introduced the idea that the atom has an internal structure. It also showed that electrical charge is an important part of atomic structure. However, the Plum Pudding Model still had a weakness: it did not include a nucleus. Later experiments showed that positive charge and most of the atom’s mass are concentrated in a very small central region.



Rutherford’s Nuclear Model

The next major change came from Ernest Rutherford. In 1911, Rutherford and his team performed the famous gold foil experiment. They directed alpha particles toward a very thin sheet of gold foil and observed what happened after the particles passed through or bounced away.

Most of the alpha particles passed straight through the foil. This showed that the atom is mostly empty space. However, a small number of particles were deflected, and some even bounced back. Rutherford concluded that the atom must contain a very small, dense, positively charged center. He called this central part the nucleus.



The Nuclear Model changed the understanding of the atom completely. Instead of being a solid sphere or a positively charged mass with electrons scattered inside, the atom was now understood to have a central nucleus with electrons around it. Rutherford’s model explained that most of the atom’s mass is found in the nucleus, while the rest of the atom is mostly empty space.

However, Rutherford’s model also had a problem. It did not clearly explain why electrons moving around the nucleus did not lose energy and fall into it. This question led to the development of the next atomic model.



Bohr’s Planetary Model

In 1913, Niels Bohr, who had studied under Rutherford, improved the nuclear model by explaining how electrons could remain stable around the nucleus. Bohr proposed that electrons move in fixed paths or shells around the nucleus. These paths are sometimes compared to planets orbiting the Sun, which is why the model is often called the Planetary Model or Bohr Model.




Bohr explained that each electron shell has a specific energy level. Electrons closer to the nucleus have lower energy, while electrons farther away have higher energy. An electron can move to a higher energy level if it absorbs energy. It can also return to a lower energy level by releasing energy, often in the form of light.

This model helped explain why atoms release or absorb specific amounts of energy. It was especially successful in explaining the behavior of hydrogen, which has only one electron. However, the model became less successful when scientists tried to apply it to atoms with many electrons. Because of this limitation, a more advanced model was needed.

Key Points of Bohr’s model:
• Electrons move around the nucleus in fixed orbits or shells.
• Each shell has a specific energy level.
• Electrons absorb energy when they move to a higher shell.
• Electrons release energy when they move to a lower shell.
• The model worked well for hydrogen but not for more complex atoms.



The Quantum Atomic Model

The Quantum Atomic Model is the modern model of the atom. Unlike the Bohr Model, it does not describe electrons as moving in fixed circular paths. Instead, it explains that electrons are found in regions where they are most likely to be located. These regions are called orbitals or electron clouds.

In 1926, Erwin Schrödinger used mathematical equations to describe the probable location of electrons. This means that scientists cannot say the exact position of an electron at every moment. Instead, they can describe the area where an electron is most likely to be found. This made the model more accurate than earlier atomic models.



The Quantum Model may be harder to visualize because it does not look like a simple solar system. However, it gives a better explanation of how electrons behave, especially in atoms with many electrons. It also connects atomic structure with the principles of quantum mechanics, which studies matter and energy at the atomic and subatomic levels.



WHAT HOLDS ATOMS TOGETHER?

Atoms may look simple in diagrams, but they are held together by powerful interactions between tiny particles. Inside an atom, there are protons, neutrons, and electrons. Protons and neutrons are packed together in the nucleus, while electrons are found around the nucleus in orbitals or electron clouds. The question is: if protons are all positively charged and should repel one another, why does the nucleus not break apart? The answer involves two important forces: the strong nuclear force and the electromagnetic force.

The strong nuclear force mainly explains why the nucleus stays together, while the electromagnetic force explains the attraction between the positively charged nucleus and negatively charged electrons. Together, these forces help atoms remain stable and allow matter to exist in the forms we observe every day.



Strong Force
The strong force is the force that holds the nucleus together. Inside the nucleus, there are protons and neutrons. Protons have a positive charge, so they naturally push away from other protons. This is because particles with the same charge repel each other.

However, the nucleus does not easily break apart because the strong force keeps the protons and neutrons tightly together.The strong force acts like a very powerful glue inside the nucleus. It keeps the protons and neutrons close together.

Example:
In a Lithium atom, the nucleus has 2 protons and 2 neutrons. Even though the protons repel each other, the strong force keeps the nucleus stable.



Electromagnetic Force
The The electromagnetic force is the force between charged particles. It follows this rule:
• Opposite charges attract
• Same charges repel

In an atom:
• Protons are positive
• Electrons are negative

Because protons and electrons have opposite charges, they attract each other. This attraction helps keep electrons near the nucleus. The electromagnetic force is like the attraction between opposite sides of magnets. The positive nucleus attracts the negative electrons.

IMPORTANCE OF SUBATOMIC PARTICLES
• Subatomic particles play a major role in many fields of science and technology. In physics, they help scientists understand the nature of matter and energy.

• In chemistry, the behavior of electrons explains how atoms combine to form compounds.



• In medicine, radioactive particles are used in treatments and imaging techniques such as radiation therapy and PET scans.


• In technology, the movement of electrons is essential in producing electricity and operating electronic devices.



• The study of subatomic particles also led to the development of nuclear energy, particle accelerators, and advanced scientific instruments. Scientists continue to study these particles to discover new information about the universe and the forces that govern it.

Key Points of the Importance of Subatomic Particles:
• Explains matter and energy
• Forms chemical bonds
• Used in medical technology
• Powers electronic devices
• Helps study the universe



APPLICATIONS IN REAL LIFE
Microscale Applications
It involves processes, devices, or systems that operate at extremely small dimensions, usually from micrometers (μm) to nanometers (nm). These applications are important in fields such as biology, medicine, chemistry, electronics, and nanotechnology. At this scale, scientists study cells, microorganisms, microscopic particles, and tiny electronic components that cannot be seen clearly with the naked eye.

1. Electronics and Semiconductors
Electrons are responsible for the flow of electricity in electronic devices. The movement of electrons through semiconductors allows technologies such as smartphones, computers, televisions, and calculators to operate. Transistors and microchips rely on the controlled behavior of electrons to process, store, and transfer information.


2. Medical Applications
Subatomic particles are widely used in medicine for diagnosis and treatment. Radioactive particles help doctors identify diseases through imaging techniques such as PET scans and MRI scans. Radiation therapy uses high-energy particles to destroy cancer cells while minimizing damage to healthy tissues.


3. Microscopy and Scientific Research
Electron microscopes use electrons instead of visible light to produce highly magnified images of microscopic objects. This technology enables scientists to study bacteria, viruses, cells, and tiny structures that cannot be seen with ordinary microscopes. Research involving subatomic particles also helps in developing medicines and advanced materials.


4. Chemical Reactions and Material Development
Electrons play a major role in chemical bonding and reactions. Their interactions form molecules and compounds used in medicines, fuels, plastics, and food products. Understanding electron behavior helps scientists create stronger, lighter, and more durable materials for various industries.

Key Points of Microscale Applications:
• Electronics and semiconductors
• Medical imaging and therapy
• Electron microscopy
• Nanotechnology research
• Chemical bonding and reactions



Macroscale Applications
It involves systems or phenomena that occur on a large, visible scale that can be observed directly without magnification. These applications deal with large objects, environments, or processes such as weather systems, transportation, engineering structures, and planetary phenomena. In science, the macroscale refers to large-scale observations and interactions that are measurable in everyday life.

1. Nuclear Energy
The interaction of protons and neutrons inside the atomic nucleus is used in nuclear power plants. Through nuclear fission, large amounts of energy are released and converted into electricity used in homes, schools, hospitals, and industries.


2. Communication and Technology
Modern communication systems depend on the movement of electrons and electromagnetic waves. Technologies such as satellites, radios, mobile phones, televisions, and internet systems use principles involving subatomic particles to send and receive information over long distances.


3. Space Exploration and Astronomy
Scientists study subatomic particles to understand stars, galaxies, black holes, and the origin of the universe. Nuclear fusion inside stars, including the Sun, occurs because of interactions among subatomic particles. Particle physics also helps explain cosmic radiation and other astronomical phenomena.


4. Industrial and Engineering Applications
Industries use radiation and particle-based technologies in manufacturing and engineering. Radiation helps inspect materials for cracks or defects, sterilize medical equipment, preserve food, and improve product quality. Engineers also apply principles of atomic and particle physics in transportation, construction, and energy systems.

Key Points of Macroscale Applications:
• Nuclear power generation
• Communication systems
• Space exploration
• Industrial radiation technology
• Engineering and manufacturing
